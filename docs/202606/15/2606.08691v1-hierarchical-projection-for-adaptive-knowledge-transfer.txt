## Abstract 

Modern data-driven applications increasingly involve learning from multiple het-erogeneous sources, where a target dataset is limited but related information is available across domains. Naively combining these sources can degrade perfor-mance when relevance varies or spurious signals are present, posing a fundamental challenge for trustworthy cross-domain learning. We propose Projection Transfer Learning (ProjectionTL), a unified framework that integrates hierarchical Bayesian modeling with adaptive projection for selective knowledge transfer. The key idea is to decouple transfer at two levels: first, we construct a source-guided hierar-chical prior that aggregates information across sources using data-driven weights, capturing global alignment between each source and the target; second, we refine this borrowing through a posterior-projection step that operates at the feature level, selectively retaining coordinates that exhibit local agreement with the target signal. This two-stage design enables the method to simultaneously perform source selec-tion and feature selection, thereby mitigating negative transfer while preserving interpretability. ProjectionTL provides a principled approach to integrating hetero-geneous data across domains, bridging statistical modeling and modern machine learning paradigms for robust and interpretable transfer. Through simulations and real-world biomedical applications, we demonstrate improved accuracy, sta-bility, and interpretability compared to existing methods. Our framework offers a scalable and generalizable strategy for trustworthy cross-domain learning in high-dimensional settings. 

## 1 Introduction 

In many scientific domains, it is increasingly common to have multiple heterogeneous datasets that partially overlap in features or modalities, yet share a common prediction task. These datasets, or sources, may vary in quality, scale, and completeness, leading to challenges in jointly learning from them without being misled by uninformative or misaligned sources. Our objective is to develop a principled transfer learning framework that selectively borrows information from multiple sources to improve estimation and prediction in a target dataset, while maintaining robustness to heterogeneity and block-wise missingness. Transfer learning in high-dimensional linear regression has gained popularity with Bastani [2021] who considered a single source. This was extended to multiple sources via Trans-Lasso by Li et al. [2022]. Extensions to GLM succeeded soon [Li et al., 2024, Tian and Feng, 2023]. These methods have several challenges. Firstly, they require individual-level data, which is almost infeasible nowadays owing to data-sharing constraints, thereby leading to federated learning algorithms [Li et al., 2023, Lu et al., 2024]. Secondly, these methods assume complete overlap of the feature space between the target and all sources. Consequently, they may suffer from negative transfer when only subsets of features are shared or when sources exhibit heterogeneous, coordinate-specific deviations

from the target. Some works address this limitation of heterogeneous and non-overlapping sources [Duan et al., 2022, Zhao et al., 2023, Chang et al., 2024, Zhao et al., 2023, Pal et al., 2026, Asiaee and Pal, 2026, Ghosh et al., 2026]. In many contemporary applications, these challenges arise naturally. For example, in biomedical studies, multi-center cohorts often collect overlapping but non-identical modalities, such as imaging, genomics, and clinical variables, with substantial variation in measurement protocols and population composition. Similarly, in policy evaluation and real-world evidence generation, historical datasets may differ in covariate availability or data quality, yet contain partially relevant signals for a current target population. These settings require methods that can adaptively borrow information at a finer granularity than the dataset level, while remaining robust to structured heterogeneity and missingness. Most of the above discussed approaches rely on ℓ1 or ℓ2-norm based distance metrics that capture the amount of agreement between the sources and the target. This is not ideal as sources and target models can differ in scale or structure. To address this, Gu et al. [2024] devised a cosine-similarity based distance metric and later Lai et al. [2026] developed a Bayesian transfer learning algorithm called TRADER that uses this new metric to transfer knowledge from sources along with the horseshoe prior [Carvalho et al., 2009] for sparsity. Although these methods can borrow adaptively from different sources with different strengths of similarity with the target, they assume complete overlap of covariates and complete match of signals. [Zhang et al., 2024], on the other hand, proposed CONCERT, which is a Bayesian high-dimensional transfer-learning method that uses covariate-specific spike-and-slab priors to decide which variables should borrow information from which sources, so it can transfer only partially shared signals instead of relying on one global source-target similarity measure. Although CONCERT allows for covariate-level partial transfer, it does not explicitly model source-level heterogeneity through adaptive source weights like TRADER does; that is, once a covariate is deemed transferable, the method does not separately learn how strongly each auxiliary source should contribute to that covariate. To date, there is limited methodological development for transfer learning algorithms that can 

simultaneously (1) adaptively borrow across heterogeneous sources with varying degrees of relevance and (2) selectively borrow across heterogeneous covariate components within each source. In practice, such scenarios arise quite commonly: different institutions, imaging centers, or cohorts may measure partially overlapping modalities, or collect heterogeneous but related variables that only coincide in some subsets of features. Our Bayesian approach, called Projection Transfer Learning (ProjectionTL), targets exactly this gap and combines the two forms of adaptivity: a hierarchical prior performs source-level borrowing , learning which source models are globally aligned with the target, while a posterior-projection step performs component-level borrowing , selectively retaining coordinates that remain transferable after accounting for heterogeneous sparsity across sources. The second projection step for component-level borrowing is motivated by recent developments on projection-based inference and variable selection, where dense posterior estimates are projected onto sparse representations to improve interpretability and stability [Wang and Ghosal, 2023b,a, Chakraborty and Ghosal, 2022, Pal and Ghosal, 2025, 2024, Pal and Ghoshal, 2024, Gili et al., 2026]. These approaches provide strong theoretical guarantees and computational advantages, but they do not explicitly incorporate external source information or address the challenges of multi-source heterogeneity. In particular, existing projection-posterior methods operate within a single dataset and are not designed to disentangle transferable and non-transferable components across multiple sources. Our contributions are fourfold. First, we introduce a novel hierarchical projection framework that enables adaptive, component-wise transfer learning under heterogeneous and partially overlapping feature spaces. Second, we develop a principled weighting mechanism based on validation-informed similarity that quantifies source relevance while allowing for a “no-transfer” fallback. Our approach does not require individual-level data and can work with only summary information from sources. Third, we establish theoretical guarantees for posterior contraction in high-dimensional settings, ex-plicitly accounting for both informative and non-informative source regimes. Finally, we demonstrate through simulations and real data applications that the proposed method achieves improved estima-tion accuracy, variable selection, and robustness compared to existing transfer learning approaches, particularly in settings with partial overlap, source heterogeneity, and block-wise missingness. More-over, compared with recent deep-learning safe-transfer frameworks, our high-dimensional linear formulation offers a complementary advantage: it yields interpretable coefficient-level borrowing decisions together with explicit statistical uncertainty quantification. 2The rest of the paper is organized as follows. In Section 2, we introduce the proposed ProjectionTL framework and describe its hierarchical prior and projection components. Section 3 presents theoreti-cal results on posterior contraction and model selection consistency. Section 4 evaluates performance through extensive simulation studies under varying degrees of overlap and heterogeneity. Section 5 illustrates the method on real-world data from multi-phase Alzheimer’s disease studies. Section 6 concludes with a discussion of limitations and future directions. 

## 2 Methodology 

The following sections introduce ProjectionTL, that provides a unified solution for transfer learning across multiple heterogeneous, partially overlapping, or multi-modal datasets. It automatically adjusts the degree of borrowing both across and within sources, ensuring interpretability, robustness, and improved generalization under complex real-world data integration settings.

2.4 Extension to Multi-Modality 

The ProjectionTL framework extends naturally to multi-modal settings with block-wise missing data as shown in Figure 2. Rather than requiring joint modeling of raw feature spaces across modalities, the method operates at the level of source-specific coefficient estimates, allowing information from different modalities to be integrated through the hierarchical prior. Specifically, for each modality, source-level coefficients are aggregated using the same alignment-based weighting scheme, so that sources contributing relevant modality-specific signals receive larger weights. When a modality is unobserved for a given source or cohort, the corresponding coordinates do not contribute to the prior aggregation. In the projection step, adaptive weights penalize coordinates that lack consistent support across sources and the target validation estimate, effectively restricting borrowing to observed and aligned components. 

Figure 2: Illustration of multi-modal transfer learning problem with block-missingness. This mechanism enables selective transfer across partially overlapping modalities without requir-ing imputation or explicit modeling of missing data. In settings where different cohorts provide complementary but incomplete modalities (such as neurodegenerative disease studies, multi-omics data integration, and electronic health records) the method borrows information only where signals are shared, while downweighting modality-specific noise and discrepancies. Compared to related approaches such as R2 and BR2 [Xu and Qu, 2025], which focus on multi-task learning under 5block-missingness, the proposed framework explicitly addresses transfer learning with adaptive borrowing across heterogeneous sources and modalities.

## 5 Real Data Analysis Using ADNI Data 

To evaluate the practical performance of the proposed method, we apply ProjectionTL to real-world data from the Alzheimer’s Disease Neuroimaging Initiative (ADNI), a multi-phase study spanning ADNI1, ADNI-GO, ADNI2, ADNI3, and ADNI4. The outcome of interest is cognitive function, measured by the Mini-Mental State Examination (MMSE), and predictors consist of high-dimensional imaging features related to regional amyloid-β (A β) deposition. Individual cohorts have limited sample sizes relative to the number of predictors, while earlier cohorts provide partially relevant but heterogeneous information due to differences in study design and population characteristics. This creates a natural transfer learning setting. We consider both heterogeneous-source transfer and multi-modal transfer under block-wise missingness to assess how the proposed framework adapts to realistic data integration challenges. 

5.1 Study 1: Heterogeneous Single-Model Sources 

We first consider the heterogeneous-source transfer setting, where multiple cohorts differ in their degree of relevance to the target population but share partially overlapping signal structure. The predictor matrix X consists of p = 166 regional standardized uptake value ratios (SUVRs) from amyloid PET imaging, and the response Y is the baseline MMSE score. We formulate the analysis as a transfer learning problem with one target and two sources. The target dataset consists of newly enrolled participants from a recent study phase [Weiner et al., 2017] with complete PET and MMSE measurements. The first source comprises an earlier cohort ( n1 = 128 ), which provides large-scale A β PET data but differs from the target in imaging protocols and diagnostic criteria. The second source is a larger intermediate cohort ( n2 = 769 ), which is more closely aligned with the target but still exhibits heterogeneity due to evolving study design and cohort composition. These differences induce partial alignment in the underlying amyloid-to-cognition relationship, making this a representative setting for heterogeneous multi-source transfer learning. We next consider a multi-modal transfer setting with block-wise missingness. ## 6 Discussion 

This paper proposes ProjectionTL, a two-stage framework that combines source-guided Bayesian pri-ors with projection-based sparsification to address heterogeneous multi-source transfer learning. The central contribution lies in decoupling where to borrow (through source-level weighting via a Dirichlet prior) from what to borrow (through component-wise projection), enabling adaptive and interpretable transfer under partial overlap and block-wise missingness. This separation is conceptually clean and practically effective, as reflected in improved estimation accuracy and support recovery across simulations and real data analyses. Empirically, the simulation results demonstrate that ProjectionTL achieves consistent improvements in estimation error and support recovery. Importantly, the results also show that the method is robust in the absence of informative sources, indicating effective control of negative transfer. The real data analysis further illustrates the method’s applicability in complex biomedical settings, where data are collected across multiple phases with varying protocols and partially overlapping features. Several aspects of the framework highlight opportunities for further refinement and extension. The projection step induces sparsity through ℓ1 regularization, which provides stability and interpretability in high-dimensional settings, though alternative projection schemes or debiasing strategies could further enhance estimation of large signals. As future directions, we are developing formal model-selection guarantees and post-projection uncertainty quantification through the technique of debiasing. In addition, the current formulation focuses on linear models, which serve as a foundational setting for high-dimensional transfer learning; extending the framework to accommodate nonlinear relationships and more complex structures would broaden its applicability in modern data settings. 9References

## A Additional Information 

Transfer learning is most useful precisely when the target sample size is limited; hence, removing a non-negligible fraction of the target observations to form a separate validation set can substantially degrade the quality of the target-only pilot estimate. Since this pilot enters downstream through the cosine similarities between the target and source estimates, the rescaling of the source estimates, and the resulting Dirichlet-weighted prior mean, a noisy or unrepresentative validation estimate can adversely affect the entire information-borrowing mechanism. For this reason, we compute the target-only pilot estimate using the full target dataset and use it only to construct the borrowing weights and prior hyperparameters. To assess whether this full-target pilot introduces harmful overfitting, we compare the in-sample and out-of-sample prediction errors under the full-target pilot and the validation-set pilot. As shown in Figure 5, the average out-of-sample prediction MSE minus in-sample prediction MSE, is smaller for the full-target pilot than for the validation-set pilot ( 0.146 

versus 0.240 ). Thus, using the full target sample to guide the prior does not inflate the apparent performance; if anything, it yields a more stable borrowing rule with better out-of-sample behavior. To assess whether reusing the full target dataset (for both informing the conjugate prior mean and posterior updating) introduces harmful optimistic bias, we conduct a simulation under a slightly modified data-generating process compared to Section 4 with n0 = 150 , p = 200 , s = 20 , ω = 0 .8,

σ = 1 , and bmag = 0 .5, running 100 independent replications each fitted with two configurations of ProjectionTL: the proposed full-target informed prior mean, which uses all n0 observations for both prior construction and the likelihood, and a carved out validation set, which reserves 30% of the target observations exclusively for guiding the Dirichlet prior on the weights through the cosine similarity, and uses the remaining 70% for the likelihood. We compute the out-of-sample minus in-sample prediction mean squared error evaluated on an independent test set of ntest = 500 observations, which quantifies overfitting in the sense that a larger gap indicates the model fits the training data more optimistically than it fits new data. The full-target based implementation yields a mean generalization gap of 0.146 compared to 0.240 for the validation-set based approach (Figure 5), with the distribution of per-replication differences concentrated below zero in [X]% of replications and a paired Wilcoxon test strongly failing to reject the null in the direction of double-use harm ( p = [ X]). We conclude that reusing the target data for pilot estimation does not introduce optimistic bias; rather, the split pilot exhibits larger overfitting due to its reduced likelihood sample size of 0.7n0 = 105 < p , which places the model in a harder estimation regime. 

## B Sensitivity Analysis for the Tuning Parameter 

To address the concern that performance may hinge on the choice of the projection tuning parameter 

λ, we vary it over the grid {0.25 λCV , 0.5λCV , λ CV , 2λCV , 4λCV }, where λCV is selected by cross-validation on the target data using the adaptive weights wj from Equation (2.5) . As shown in Figure 6, the five ProjectionTL variants produce nearly identical MSE and MCC curves across all overlap ratios, demonstrating that performance is not sensitive to λ within a fourfold range above and below the cross-validated value. This robustness is attributable to the coordinate-specific weights wj , which govern which variables are selected by concentrating penalty reduction on coordinates where sources agree with the target pilot; the global λ controls only the overall sparsity level and is thus secondary to the adaptive weighting mechanism in determining the transfer-protection trade-off.
